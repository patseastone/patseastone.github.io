Book Description

You’ve decided to start going to the gym. Or to start again. Either way, the hardest part isn’t the weights — it’s the not‑knowing.

The Complete Handbook is the gym guide that treats you like an adult. No mandatory exercises. No bro‑science. No rigid twelve‑week programme that assumes your body works exactly like everyone else’s. Instead, it gives you the principles that underpin every effective training programme — and the freedom to build something that actually fits your life.

Inside you’ll find a Quick Start Card for walking in tomorrow, over 70 illustrated exercises with “if not this, then that” alternatives, a session builder that puts you in control, and a gentle roadmap for your first six months. Whether you’re walking through the door for the first time, or returning after years away and wanting to lift on your own terms — this is where you start.

Author Bio

Pat Seastone is a fitness writer dedicated to making the weight room accessible. Disillusioned by bro‑science, aggressive fitness culture, and rigid 12‑week programmes, Pat writes warm, permission‑first books that trust beginners to make their own choices. The Complete Handbook is Pat’s definitive guide to lifting on your own terms.

Sample Q&A / Interview Questions

1. What made you write a beginner’s gym guide?

Because most gym guides assume you already know the basics — or they hand you a rigid programme that doesn’t account for your body, your preferences, or your life. I wanted to write something that treats beginners like adults, not like recruits. Something that says: here are the principles, now build something that works for you.

2. Who is this book for?

Anyone who’s ever stood at the gym door and thought, “I don’t know what to do.” Complete beginners, returning lifters who’ve been away for years, people who were told to start strength training by a physio or doctor and had no idea where to begin. It’s also for people who tried a rigid programme and quit because it didn’t fit their life.

3. Why no rigid plans?

Rigid plans work — until they don’t. Life gets in the way. You miss a session, you feel behind, you quit. This book gives you the principles behind every effective programme so you can build your own sessions around the exercises you actually enjoy. You’re in control.

4. What’s the most common mistake beginners make?

Doing too much, too soon. Beginners don’t need to train six days a week or follow advanced techniques. The best programme for a beginner is the one they can stick to. Two or three sessions a week, consistent effort, and patience. Everything else is noise.

5. How does your book handle exercise alternatives?

Every exercise in the book has an “If not this, then that” section. If you hate barbell rows, here’s a machine that does the same job. If you can’t do pull‑ups yet, here’s the lat pulldown and the assisted machine. You should never feel stuck doing something you hate.

6. Why did you publish under a pseudonym?

Because the book isn’t about me. It’s about the reader. Using a pen name keeps the focus on the content — on the principles, the exercises, and the quiet satisfaction of getting stronger. I’m not interested in building a personal brand. I’m interested in writing useful books.

7. What role does the Quick Start Card play?

It’s the absolute minimum you need to walk into a gym today and leave feeling like you did something right. Pick one push, one pull, one leg exercise. Do two warm‑up sets, two working sets. Go home. You’ve started. The rest of the book is there when you’re ready.

8. How long does it take to see results?

It depends what you mean by results. You’ll feel more confident within a few sessions. You’ll notice the weight moving more smoothly within a few weeks. Visible muscle changes take months. The book encourages you to track progress in multiple ways — not just the mirror.

9. What’s your writing process?

I think deeply about what beginners actually need to know, then I write it as clearly as I can. No jargon. No hype. I spent months refining every chapter — stripping out anything that felt like noise, keeping only what’s genuinely useful. The illustrations were a massive part of the process too. Every exercise is shown start‑to‑end so you can actually see what you’re meant to do.

10. Do you need a gym membership to use this book?

Most of the exercises use standard gym equipment — machines, cables, free weights. But there are bodyweight options too, and the principles apply anywhere. If you’re training at home with limited kit, the book will still help you understand how to structure your sessions and progress over time.

11. What’s the single most important piece of advice you’d give a beginner?

Show up. That’s it. Don’t worry about optimising your programme or finding the perfect split. Walk in, warm up, move something with control, come back and do it again. Everything else flows from that.

12. Are you planning more books?

I have ideas, yes. But I’m focused on this book for now — making sure it reaches the people who need it. If there’s a genuine gap that another book could fill, I’ll write it. But only if it’s truly useful.
